# Making Old-School Fresh — Youth Appeal Research

## Brands That Successfully Rebirthed Heritage

### Fitness/Sport
- **Hyrox** (est. 2017): Combined old-school functional fitness (rowing, sled push, farmers carry) with **race-format competition**. Made garage-gym movements feel like **Formula 1 for fitness**. Key: elevated the presentation, not the activity
- **NOBULL** (est. 2015): Training shoes brand. Made CrossFit gear feel **streetwear-cool** instead of garage-gym-ugly. Key: stripped down design, no logos, raw materials (SuperFabric)
- **Onnit**: Made kettlebells into **art pieces** (zombie, chimp, darth vader bells). Key: treated training tools as cultural objects
- **Tonal**: Made strength training feel like **a Tesla on your wall**. Key: technology wrapper around basic exercises

### Non-Fitness Examples
- **Carhartt WIP**: Workwear brand (1889) became **streetwear icon** without changing core products. Key: context shift (construction site → skate park), collaborations with artists, limited drops
- **New Balance**: "Dad shoe" → most coveted sneaker brand among Gen Z. Key: embraced the "uncool" authenticity, collaborated with cultural tastemakers (JJJJound, Aimé Leon Dore)
- **Patagonia**: Outdoor heritage → **environmental activism brand**. Key: stood for something bigger than products
- **Iron Path / Rogue Fitness**: Made garage gym equipment feel like **precision engineering**. Key: quality obsession, brutalist aesthetic
- **Stanley Cup** (the tumbler): 110-year-old brand became **Gen Z obsession** in 2023-24. Key: authentic product quality + community + limited colorways

### What Works
1. **Don't change the product, change the context** — Put the old-school thing in a new environment
2. **Embrace rawness** — Don't over-polish. Youth reads "too polished" as corporate/fake
3. **Community > marketing** — Build tribe, not audience
4. **Cultural crossover** — Music, art, fashion intersections make sport feel like culture
5. **Scarcity and drops** — Limited releases create desire
6. **Stand for something** — Values > features

## Gen Z Design Preferences (Research-Based)
- **Anti-corporate**: Distrust polished brand identities. Prefer raw, authentic, slightly rough
- **Nostalgia + futurism**: Y2K aesthetic, brutalist web design, retro-futurism
- **Texture**: Halftone, dither, grain, noise — imperfection signals authenticity
- **Typography**: Sharp, angular, experimental (the Enigma typeface reference fits)
- **Color**: High-contrast, unexpected combinations (earth + neon, not pastel)
- **Community**: Logos become **symbols of belonging** (like patches, tattoos, stickers)

## Application to PIDYOM
- Kettlebells are inherently "old-school" → this is an **asset**, not a problem
- The raw cast iron texture → **brand texture** (halftone, grain, dithered effects)
- "Getting up" (the Turkish Get-Up) is the most **spectacular** kettlebell movement → make it the brand's signature
- Ukrainian military command "Підйом!" = **wake up call** → brand as alarm clock for a generation sitting too much
- The simplicity of one tool (kettlebell) vs complexity of gym memberships → **anti-establishment fitness**

---

> **Key insight**: PIDYOM doesn't need to convince youth that kettlebells are cool. It needs to show that **simplicity IS the rebellion** — while everyone buys gym memberships, tracking 47 machines, paying for fancy equipment... PIDYOM says: "pick up this iron ball. Everything you need is in this one tool and your own body."
